Section 1: What is AI Governance
1

What is AI Governance

AI Governance is the set of policies, processes, roles and technologies that ensure the responsible, safe and compliant use of artificial intelligence systems within an organization. In the agentic context, governance goes beyond models — it encompasses autonomous agents that make decisions, execute actions and interact with external systems.

Policies

Explicit rules about what agents can and cannot do. What data they access, what actions they perform, who approves what.

Processes

Approval flows, review cycles, periodic audits. How an agent is created, tested, approved, monitored, and retired.

Technology

Observability tools, immutable records, access control, sandboxing and automatic alerts that enforce policies.

Why Governance Matters Now

In 2023, less than 5% of companies had formal AI Governance policies. In 2025, regulations such as the EU AI Act, BACEN guidelines on automated credit models and LGPD requirements made this mandatory. Companies without governance face:

  • •Regulatory fines (EU AI Act: up to 3% of global turnover)
  • •Incidents of autonomous agents making incorrect decisions without auditing
  • •Shadow AI: teams using unapproved agents with sensitive data
  • •Inability to explain automated decisions to customers or regulators

Governance vs. Bureaucracy

The goal of governance is not to stop innovation — it is to create guardrails that allow you to move quickly and safely. The difference:

Bureaucracy Good Governance
Manual approval for each prompt Automatic approval for low-risk agents, manual only for high-risk agents
Retroactive documentation that no one reads Automatic registration via code (IaC + GitOps)
Committee that only says no Committee that provides templates, standards and accelerates adoption
Outdated annual review Continuous monitoring with automatic alerts
Section 2: AI Adoption Policy
2

AI Adoption Policy

An AI adoption policy defines the rules of the game: who can create agents, with what data, for what purposes, and with what approvals. It must be clear enough to guide, but flexible enough not to hamper teams.

Structure of an Effective Policy

1
Scope and Definitions

What counts as an “AI agent” in the organization (LLM with tools, ML automations, recommendation systems with autonomy). Precise definition avoids ambiguity about what needs approval.

2
Risk Classification

Agents are classified as Low / Medium / High risk. Each level has different approval, monitoring and periodic review requirements.

3
Acceptable Use of Data

Which categories of data can be sent to external models (OpenAI, Anthropic) and which should be on-premises or in self-hosted models. Identifiable customer data = on-premises or specific agreement.

4
Responsibility and Accountability

For each agent in production, an identified human is the "Agent Owner" — responsible for monitoring, incidents and deactivation. No owner = no deploy.

Allowed by Default (Low Risk)

  • ✓Text suggestions for users to review before sending
  • ✓Classification of non-sensitive internal documents
  • ✓Support chatbots with curated FAQ
  • ✓Code generation for human review
  • ✓Analysis of anonymized aggregated data

Requires Approval (High Risk)

  • ✗Agents with access to personally identifiable data
  • ✗Automatic decisions that affect customers (credit, price)
  • ✗Running code in production without human review
  • ✗Autonomous external communication on behalf of the company
  • ✗Access to financial or HR systems

Fighting Shadow AI

Shadow AI — teams using ChatGPT, Claude, or unapproved agents with enterprise data — is the biggest governance risk in 2025. The answer is not an outright ban (ineffective), but rather:

Easy Way

Offer an approved and easy-to-use platform. If the official path is easier, no one needs the shadow.

Detection

Monitor network traffic for unapproved LLM APIs. DLP (Data Loss Prevention) to detect sensitive data leaving.

Amnesty + Registration

Create an amnesty period for teams to declare shadow agents and migrate them to the official platform without punishment.

Section 3: AI Governance Framework
3

AI Governance Framework

A governance framework needs three operational components: a committee that decides, a registry that tracks, and approval processes that operate at a speed compatible with the business.

AI Committee (AI Council)

👑 Chief AI Officer/Sponsor

Final decision in high-risk cases and disputes. Ensures strategic alignment with business objectives.

🔒 CISO (Security)

Assess security risks for each agent. Approves sandboxing and access control architecture.

⚖️ DPO/Legal

Ensures compliance with LGPD, BACEN, EU AI Act. Evaluates legal implications of automated decisions.

🛠️ CTO/Principal Architect

Evaluates technical feasibility, integration risks and compliance with the company's architectural standards.

📊 Business Representative

Validates the use case, expected KPIs, and impact for end users. Avoid technical solutions without real problems.

🔬 Ethics / Responsible AI

Assesses algorithmic bias, impact on vulnerable groups, explainability of decisions. Growing role in mature companies.

Agent Registry

Every agent in production must have a canonical record. This is the organization's "AI inventory" — equivalent to the CMDB for infrastructure.

Approval Flow by Risk Level

LOW
Self-approval + automatic registration. Team lead signs. Deploy in 1 day. Review every 6 months.
AVERAGE
Security approval (CISO) + DPO. Mandatory registration. Deploy within 5 days. Review every 3 months.
HIGH
Full AI Council approval. Mandatory HITL. Immutable audit trail. Deploy within 15 days. Review every 1 month.
Section 4: Risk Assessment Framework

Risk Assessment for Agents

Risk Assessment is the structured process of evaluating an agent's risk level before approving its deployment. Use the 4 dimensions below to calculate the risk score:

🗂️

Dimension 1: Data

Public data only +0
Anonymized internal data +1
Personal data (LGPD) +2
Sensitive data (health, financial) +3
⚡

Dimension 2: Actions

Read/Review only +0
Writing to internal systems +1
External communication +2
Financial/legal transactions +3
💰

Dimension 3: Financial Impact

No direct impact +0
Internal impact < R$10k +1
Impact on customers < R$100k +2
Systemic impact > R$100k +3
↩️

Dimension 4: Reversibility

Fully reversible +0
Reversible with effort +1
Difficult to reverse +2
Irreversible +3

Final Score → Risk Level

4–7
Medium Risk

8–12

Section 5: Change Management
5

Change Management — Cultural Transformation

Technology is the easy part. The biggest challenge in adopting enterprise AI is cultural transformation. Teams resist due to fear of replacement, distrust in results and lack of skill. The ADKAR model offers a framework to navigate this.

ADKAR for Agentic Transformation

A
Awareness

Why is change necessary? Communicate the business problem, not the technological solution. “Competitors are responding to prospects 10x faster” resonates more than “let’s implement AI agents.”

D
Desire — Desire

People need to want to change. Show "what's in store for me": less repetitive work, time for more interesting activities. Avoid “AI will make you more productive” narratives — focus on “AI will eliminate boring tasks.”

K
Knowledge

How to use the new tools? Hands-on training, not slides. Internal champions who teach in the corridors. Contextual documentation within the tools themselves.

A
Ability

Ability to do it in practice. Offer sandbox for risk-free experimentation. Internal hackathons. Mentoring technical teams for business teams. Measure and celebrate first uses.

R
Reinforcement

Sustain the change. Recognize and reward adoption. Usage metrics in leadership dashboards. Internal communities of practice (AI CoP). Sharing success stories all-hands.

AI Champions Program

AI Champions are high-performing employees (not necessarily technical) who become internal ambassadors for AI adoption within their teams. They are more effective than top-down training because:

Peer-to-Peer Credibility

Colleagues trust people who do the same work more than they trust external experts or managers.

Domain Context

They know exactly which team tasks are candidates for automation and how to adapt agents.

Organic Scale

1 champion per team of 10 people = much faster adoption than a central CoE team trying to reach everyone.

Quick Wins: The First Wins Strategy

Quick wins are essential for overcoming initial skepticism and building momentum. Choose projects with visible impact in 2–4 weeks:

  • 1Meeting Summary: Agent who transcribes and summarizes meetings. Immediate impact, zero risk, high adoption.
  • 2Ticket classification: Automatic support triage. Measurable in days, visible improvement for the CS team.
  • 3Report generator: Weekly sales report automatically generated from existing data. Saves 2h/week per person.
  • 4Smart FAQ: Internal chatbot for HR, IT, financial policies. Reduces interruptions from the internal support team.

The agentic maturity model describes an organization's journey from casual use of LLMs to enterprise orchestration of multiple autonomous agents. Use it to diagnose where your business is and plan for the next level.
